  • Title: Residue

    Year: 2016

    Running Time: 11 minutes (loop)

    Additional Details:

    The installation involved two projects. On the main wall there are acrylic paintings that sync up to an 11 minute video on loop. In the entrance, there are two life sized figures trapped in chain link frames. The clinking sounds from their struggle to get out fill the gallery space. All cinematography, sound recording, prop and installation building, paintings, were made by Juan Flores. The installation features classmates as actors traversing the wall, and dancers improvising and interpreting a trapped figure.

  • Title: Post-Tw.In.Yo.Go.Fa (Post-Data)

    Year: 2019

    Running Time: Generative

    Additional Details:

    First two minutes of video is enough to watch. This installation was made in collaboration with sound artist Isaac Medina (Mexico City, Mexico) under the alias of our collective PHAGES. My role was in creating a program written in Java that uses image processing algorithms to deconstruct images taken from screen captures of social media with a particular set of hashtags from the arts festival. The program also had projection mapping features with OSC communication between a Pure Data patch that uses granular synthesis made by Isaac. The title alludes to an imagined life after Twitter, Instagram, Youtube, Google, and Facebook. Documentation of installation was made by Isaac Medina.

  • Title: Post-Data

    Year: 2019

    Running Time: Generative

    Additional Details:

    A minute of viewing is enough to watch. This is another iteration of Post-Data and gives a good idea of how the Java written program worked. Blobs based on color similarity and size are detected in a screen capture of videos online taken in real time. These blobs are placed in a particular display style on a canvas. The sound featured is the work of Mexican City based artist Isaac Medina, who uses a Pure Data patch that takes the real time audio and processes it using granular synthesis.

Statement of Purpose

My first hands-on experiences were at 15 helping create murals around San Antonio, TX. The concepts behind them were almost always inspired by the area they were installed in, looking at the Mexican-American culture, along with its history and significant contributions. This has always stuck with me.

As an undergrad at Connecticut College, aware of being a minority for the first time, I started to become familiar with the feeling of duality. This feeling grew stronger during my many yearly visits to the border towns of Del Rio, TX, where I was born, and Ciudad Acuña, Mexico, where my family is from including both my parents. One summer, I did an assistantship with installation artist Adriana Corral, whose work attracted me for its handling of the border as a topic, as well as addressing erased histories of injustice and violence. I gained research skills by digging into the archives, and I learned the importance of collecting the physical materials of the site, even those under one's nose such as soil and discarded objects.

Inspired by our many conversations, I studied abroad in Berlin, Germany, where I wanted to investigate the Berlin Wall as another example of a separation barrier. During this time, striking images of refugees started appearing from the European migrant crisis of 2015. I remember being in disbelief of how these harsh realities can look so theatrical, especially in Melilla and Ceuta, spanish cities bordering Morocco, where migrants charged in bulk to climb the border fence. That summer, I worked at Daniel Canogar's studio in Madrid, Spain. His video installations had inspired me the year before when I saw his Small Data series at Bitforms Gallery during a trip to NYC. I was interested in how he used projections to interact with real objects and cross thresholds. Working there gave me the technical confidence to create my own installation the following year that solidified themes that I continue to explore today.

For my installation, I built tall chain link fences to represent the in-between spaces that one experiences in travel, crossings, relocation, and displacement between two worlds, lives, or bodies. After seeing refugees hang their laundry on these fences, and seeing the tattered and bloodied clothes left behind by those who attempted to climb the spiked metallic structures, clothing became something that represented struggle and lost and forgotten lives. Unknowingly at first, the process of making it was a sort of private and personal cathartic performance. In the construction of the props used for climbing and shooting, I was frequently seen wearing my father's construction foreman vest and hardhat, reflecting on his immigrant journey and presence as an electrician. This realization inspired me to document it and create a separate set of pieces using a telephone pole as another climbable object, built to resemble a cross, alluding to my catholic upbringing. Using the Stations of the Cross narrative, the border became to mean a zone of death and rebirth, keeping in mind that I was the new life and continuation of my immigrant parent's work.

The performance bug grew in Mexico City, where I stayed for a year and a half thanks to a travelling fellowship to focus on sound and researching indigenous instruments. Though experimental, I continued to use found objects such as recycled electronics while exploring Post-Humanism. While there, I performed with a local sound artist who is still my biggest collaborator to this day. Working under our moniker PHAGES, we continue to present programming intensive audiovisual work in arts festivals that explore digital spaces.

At SAIC, I would like to continue to work with the moving image in relation to 3D objects and public spaces, with an interest in tying in physical computing and the web to add interactivity. Getting to work with faculty like Nick Briz and Lee Blalock would be well aligned to pick up the work I have been doing recently, as well as using the resources offered like the Electronics Lab and The Black Box would be indispensable for experimentation. Since San Antonio is behind in art with coding, one of my goals is to establish a space that exhibits and teaches cheap and accessible ways to create new-media art.
